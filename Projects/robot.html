IDP - Autonomous Robot

February - March 2023 - Coursework

Objective

Work as a team to design, manufacture and program an autonomous robot to meet the following criteria:

  • Start in a controlled manner (e.g using a button)
  • Traverse a ramp and tunnel
  • Transfer blocks from junctions to their corresponding box
  • Correctly identify and indicate (via LED) blocks by colour
  • The robot must be contrusted robustly and safely (no exposed wires or parts glued together)

The winning design is the one which accrues the most points. Points are awarded for the following:

  • Entering the area from the start box
  • Successfully traversing the ramp and the tunnel
  • Returning a block from the junctions
  • Correctly identifying the block by colour
  • Returning to the start box before 5 minutes have elapsed

Comments

I was part of the software subteam, programming a modified Arduino circuit board in C++ to control the autonomous robot. We took a modular approach, breaking tasks into smaller sub-tasks and integrating them into a cohesive system. It was important to using logical reasoning and algorithmic thinking to create: a line-following function for arena navigation, a 'block acquisition' function for collecting blocks, and a 'block return' function for placing them into the correct corresponding box. I thought to implement modulo counting logic to track the robot's location in the arena at any given moment.

We developed our management skills by deliberate planning our project timeline with a Gantt chart, setting clear objectives for testing, and making every effort to communication effectively and clearly between interdependent subteams - mechanical, electrical and software in all. Our team's strength shone through as we quickly developed a working prototype within the first three days, allowing us to concentrate on testing, debugging, and refiningg our robot. This accelerated our progress enormously.

While we achieved success in testing, our final assessment didn't meet our target. I attribute this to practicing on a dummy arena and not that which would be used in our final test. This taught me the significance of using the actual test arena, even in testing, especially considering we knew our sensors were especially sensitive to their operating conditions.

This project was so much fun and certainly my favourite work since joining Cambridge! It has driven me to look more closely at robotics and perhaps join the CU Robotics Society.
